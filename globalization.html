Russia's Information Campaign During the War in Ukraine

Narrative Control as a Weapon

In the shadows of the internet, Russia's "Informatsionnaya voyna" (information war) unfolds as a multi-pronged strategy beyond traditional cyber warfare. Their digital crusade involves controlling undersea communication cables, sculpting national narratives, and manipulating the news cycle, while constantly flooding Russia's information space with an army of bots and trolls. In Russia, a tight grip on media allows the state to sway public opinion effectively. The Kremlin's narrative, consistently broadcasted for years before the war, frames its actions in Ukraine as a protective measure against so-called Ukrainian Fascists (Nazis). Their deep-rooted, long-term narrative strategy, built on repetition and familiarity has recently proven to be particularly effective.

Under the presidency of former-Ukrainian president Victor Yanukovych, a pro-Russian leader, Russia's information war against Ukraine became more conspicuous. In February 2014, as tensions escalated, Russia's strategy of denial and obfuscation reached an almost absurd point. Despite the obvious military maneuvers threatening Ukraine, Russia continually denied any involvement of the Black Sea Fleet in destabilizing Crimea. President Putin himself characterized the armed men attempting to seize the Crimean parliament as being merely "local self-defense forces." This era, marked by Yanukovych's presidency, gives another example of Russia's adeptness at using misinformation and denial in its continued effort to reframe aggressive actions as mere attempts at peace and self-defense.
